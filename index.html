Is Cutting Carbon Dioxide Emissions Enough? A Deeper Look at Wildlife Conservation

Our Question:

What are the main factors of climate change for Amphibians and Reptiles going extinct?

When people think about climate change, they often picture rising temperatures or increasing CO₂ levels—but climate change is far more complex than that. It also drives habitat loss, alters ecosystems, and reshapes how species survive and interact.



Our Visualization

Different species respond to these pressures in very different ways: some are more sensitive to heat, others to land-use change or pollution.
In this project, you’ll explore how temperature rise, CO₂ concentration, and habitat loss interact to shift extinction risk for amphibians and reptiles. By adjusting these factors yourself, you can see how multiple forces of climate change combine to threaten biodiversity.

Rising Temperatures

As temperature increases, reptiles are affected more strongly than amphibians. This is because reptiles rely more heavily on external heat to regulate their body functions, where even small temperature changes can disrupt their feeding, reproduction, and survival.
In the simulation, we see that reptile species shift into higher risk categories faster than amphibians as temperature rises.

Habitat Loss

Habitat loss impacts both amphibians and reptiles in a similar way. As forests, wetlands, and other natural environments disappear, both groups lose critical space for breeding, shelter, and food. This tells us that habitat loss has a very significant effect on the extinction risk of any species, regardless of their biological differences making it an important factor to consider when protecting species.
In the model, increasing habitat loss causes steady risk increases for both groups at nearly the same rate.

Increasing CO2 Levels

Rising CO₂ concentrations disproportionately affect reptiles compared to amphibians. Higher CO₂ drives larger climate shifts and ecosystem changes that strongly disrupt reptile habitats, especially in dry and open environments.
In the simulation, reptiles experience a much sharper increase in extinction risk as CO₂ levels rise.

Conclusion

Climate change does not affect all species in the same way. While reducing CO₂ emissions is critical, it alone is not enough to protect vulnerable wildlife.
Temperature rise, habitat loss, and ecosystem disruption each drive extinction risk through different pathways. This means effective conservation must address multiple pressures at once—cutting emissions, protecting habitats, and managing land use—if we want to prevent further species loss.
