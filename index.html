What Threatens Species? Build Your Own Threat Model

What are the main causes for Amphibians and Reptiles going extinct?

Introduction

When people think about climate change, they often picture rising temperatures or increasing CO₂ levels—but climate change is far more complex than that. It also drives habitat loss, alters ecosystems, and reshapes how species survive and interact. Different species respond to these pressures in very different ways: some are more sensitive to heat, others to land-use change or pollution. In this project, you’ll explore how temperature rise, CO₂ concentration, and habitat loss interact to shift extinction risk for amphibians and reptiles. By adjusting these factors yourself, you can see how multiple forces of climate change combine to threaten biodiversity.

Rising Temperatures

As temperature increases, reptiles are affected more strongly than amphibians. Because reptiles rely heavily on external heat to regulate their body functions, even small temperature changes can disrupt their feeding, reproduction, and survival. In the simulation, reptile species shift into higher risk categories faster than amphibians as temperature rises.

Habitat Loss

Habitat loss impacts both amphibians and reptiles in a similar way. As forests, wetlands, and other natural environments disappear, both groups lose critical space for breeding, shelter, and food. In the model, increasing habitat loss causes steady risk increases for both groups at nearly the same rate.

Increasing CO2 Levels

Rising CO₂ concentrations disproportionately affect reptiles compared to amphibians. Higher CO₂ drives larger climate shifts and ecosystem changes that strongly disrupt reptile habitats, especially in dry and open environments. In the simulation, reptiles experience a much sharper increase in extinction risk as CO₂ levels rise.

Conclusion

Climate change does not affect all species in the same way. While reducing CO₂ emissions is critical, it alone is not enough to protect vulnerable wildlife. Temperature rise, habitat loss, and ecosystem disruption each drive extinction risk through different pathways. This means effective conservation must address multiple pressures at once—cutting emissions, protecting habitats, and managing land use—if we want to prevent further species loss.



What have you done so far?

We have created an interactive bar chart that filters between Amphibians and Reptiles, and allows users to adjust sliders for temperature rise, habitat loss, and CO₂ increase. As users manipulate these sliders, the risk levels for each species group are updated in the visualization. This helps users understand how different environmental factors contribute to the extinction risk of these species. This will form the basis of our threat model, which will be at the end of the storytelling scroll section, where we will summarize the key insights.

What will be the most challenging of your project to design and why?

The most challenging part of our project will be using animations to enhance our visualization. We want to create smooth transitions between different states of the visualization as users adjust the sliders. Since we also plan on implementing a scroll animation, we want to make sure that these features flow seamlessly, as any jumps or lags may disrupt the user. Animations can be tricky to implement effectively, as they require "perfect" timing. If the animations are too fast or too slow, it can harm a visualization rather than enhance it. Therefore, we will need to do some testing on the timing and pacing of our animations to ensure they are effective.
